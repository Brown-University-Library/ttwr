Jean Barbault, Recueïl des plus beaux morceaux de l'antiquité romaine qui existe encore (1761) (A collection of the most beautiful morsels of Roman antiquity that still exist)

This book was printed in Rome, by Bouchard et Gravier (Libraires François rüe du Cours près de Saint Marcel, de l'imprimerie de Komarek), in 1761. This is Barbault’s first and most important work. It is, moreover, a first edition. It could be understood as another version of Giovanni Battista Piranesi’s Antichità Romane (1756). In 1756 Barbault was working on 14 plates for volumes 2 and 3 of Piranesi’s Antichità, which led him to produce this book a few years later. The Oxford Online dictionary describes the book as containing “idealized Roman landscapes,” while the dealer description of a similar book contained at Harvard’s Houghton Library describes the book as containing “artistic renderings” of Rome’s ancient ruins. This is only partially accurate, for the book could be roughly divided into two parts: architectural and sculptural. The architectural portion of the book is organized by building type: Temples (pl. 1-15), triumphal arches (pl. 16-21), theaters (pl. 22-27), columns and obelisks (pl. 28-31), baths and aqueducts (pl. 34-44) and tombs/altars (pl. 45-51). Plates 55-73 are devoted almost entirely to ancient statuary (along with a few bas-reliefs), with no historical or archaeological/architectural text accompanying them. Plates 86-87 take us outside Rome proper, featuring pictures and descriptions of several recently discovered paintings from Herculaneum (along with an image of the excavation site). Thus while the author prioritizes architecture, and inscribes his work within the grand Roman vedute genre, the book itself contains much more.

The book begins with a title page vignette depicting putti drawing and measuring various Roman monuments from antiquity. Next there is an engraved allegorical vignette, accompanying the dedication to “his Excellency, Jean François Joseph de Rochechouart.” A description of each plate helps orient the reader (now located at the back). A detailed examination of who drew and engraved each plate is illuminating in terms of complicating the idea of unitary authorship in the early modern era. The plates can be divided as follows:

Thus, while we attribute the book’s authorship to Jean Barbault, many other artists had a hand in the book. Like many early modern books, it could more accurately be conceived of as a collaboration, not a monograph. Domenico Montagu looms particularly large, engraving roughly half as many of the plates as Barbault himself. Indeed, many of the characteristically Barbault images, which contain large picturesque and romantic ruins are attributable to Montagu. In addition to the artists, the publishers Gravier and Bouchard also appear to have a hand in this. It is the publishers who dedicated the book to Jean François Joseph de Rochechouart, bishop of Laon and ambassador to the Holy See. Though we can only speculate, Barbault appears to have retained no rights over the books itself, having sold the plates outright to these publishers. The publishers may therefore have commissioned the work, or have agreed to publish it for their mutual benefit. Capitalizing on the eighteenth century fashion for large Roman vedute, made possible by Piranesi and Vasi, they may have agreed to print his plates to both make a profit themselves and help rescue Barbault from his financial troubles. They were indeed able to profit from the book. A posthumous edition was made in 1770, along with abridged editions in 1787 and 1783. There was also a German edition of this work, printed in Augsburg in 1782 by C.H. Stage. Barbault’s other major work, Les Plus beaux édifices de la Rome modern was much less successful by comparison.
